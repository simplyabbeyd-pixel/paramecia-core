# Great Meadows Grassland

## 1. Identity
- **Type:** Biome
- **Structural role:** Open commons for gathering, migration, orientation, and public belonging
- **One-sentence essence:** A wind-shaped grassland where many bodies can gather without surrendering movement, privacy, or the right to leave the center.
- **Canon status:** Core identity, gift, risk, repair logic, and Accord reading locked; detailed ecology and named settlements remain provisional

## 2. Ecological Body

### Terrain
Rolling meadow shelves, broad grazing plains, seasonal flood-grass, hedgerow margins, low shelter mounds, windbreak groves, and temporary gathering basins.

### Weather and rhythm
The region is governed by wind, horizon light, storm fronts, migration seasons, flowering pulses, and the changing height of grasses. Weather is visible long before it arrives, making anticipation part of everyday life.

### Food webs
- native grasses, clover fields, seed heads, tubers, and meadow herbs
- grazing and burrowing kin
- pollinators traveling between the Meadows and Flowering Orchard
- raptors, scavengers, insects, and decomposers maintaining open-land cycles
- seed dispersal through fur, feet, wind, and seasonal caravans

### Ecological gift
Spaciousness, collective orientation, grazing abundance, public visibility, and unforced movement.

### Ecological risk
Exposure, overstimulation, storm vulnerability, trampling, overgrazing, and the false assumption that open land is unoccupied land.

### Provisional ecological anchors
- **Listening Grass:** tall seed-bearing grass whose movement reveals wind shifts and approaching bodies
- **Shade Islands:** low groves and woven canopies intentionally maintained as retreat zones
- **Soft Paths:** routes allowed to migrate seasonally rather than being paved into permanence
- **Horizon Pools:** temporary rain basins used by migrating kin and pollinators

## 3. Kin Culture

### Cultural function
The Meadows are Triquel’s clearest public commons. They host arrival feasts, open-air councils, migration exchanges, games, dances, teaching circles, and temporary encampments.

### Representative kin
Rabbitfolk, Groundhogkin, Hedgekin, migrating grazers, pollinator visitors, Foxkin pathfinders, and traveling groups from other regions. Kin are cultural participants, not biome mascots.

### Housing
Most structures are temporary, movable, or low-profile: burrow halls, windbreak tents, wheeled kitchens, shade pavilions, foldable council rings, and grass-roofed refuge mounds.

### Work
- pasture and seed stewardship
- migration guidance
- weather reading
- temporary shelter construction
- feast logistics
- path restoration
- conflict facilitation for large gatherings

### Food and feast culture
Meals favor shared platters, grazing tables, portable ovens, meadow herbs, seed breads, roots, dairy analogues where culturally appropriate, and foods brought by visiting regions. A feast is an invitation, never a test of belonging.

### Grief and memory
The Meadows remember through absence-shaped circles, uncut grass patches, wind ribbons, and names spoken once into the horizon rather than repeated until they become possession.

### Tools and symbols
- wind ribbons
- portable shade frames
- listening staffs
- seed satchels
- circular ground cloths with deliberate openings
- the **Open Ring**, a circle that is never fully closed

## 4. Consent Field

### Hospitality rule
Invitation must include the location of shade, water, quiet edges, and exits. Being visible does not mean being available.

### Refusal forms
A guest may decline food, speech, touch, dancing, testimony, introductions, or entry into the central ring without losing welcome.

### Visibility levels
- **Public:** weather, routes, feast schedules, broad ecological knowledge
- **Threshold:** council disputes, migration negotiations, personal reasons for leaving a gathering
- **Witnessed:** grief rites, minority objections, private reconciliations
- **Protected:** refuge locations used during threat, individual retreat patterns, and knowledge that could expose vulnerable kin

### Consent signals in practice
- **Green:** join, continue, move closer
- **Yellow:** slow the gathering, clarify the invitation
- **Purple:** curious, observing, not yet participating
- **Red:** stop the interaction or clear immediate space
- **White:** rest without social demand
- **Amber:** shift from participation into aftercare or regulation

### Core rule
**Observation does not create authority.** Seeing someone in the open does not grant access to their body, story, attention, or decision.

## 5. Conflict, Repair, and Care

### Primary shadow pressures
- public belonging becoming compulsory participation
- consensus flattening quiet or minority voices
- celebration becoming sensory overload
- open sightlines becoming surveillance
- grazing abundance becoming overuse
- temporary settlements becoming territorial claims

### Repair practices
- **Listening Circles:** councils where the quietest or least represented position is heard before closure
- **Shaded Retreats:** protected edge spaces that remain socially connected but free from performance
- **The Unjoined Place:** an intentionally empty seat or open patch proving that absence can remain included
- **Minority Wind:** a formal pause triggered when one participant believes the group is moving too quickly
- **Restoration Grazing:** rotating access and seasonal closures to prevent the commons from being consumed by its popularity

### Mediators
Meadow stewards, Rabbitfolk listeners, Hedgekin boundary tenders, weather readers, traveling Accord witnesses, and guest advocates from visiting regions.

### Repair metrics
The Meadows ask:
- Did quieter voices affect the outcome?
- Could someone leave without punishment?
- Were shade, water, and rest available before distress?
- Did the land recover after the gathering?
- Did visitors contribute without becoming indebted?

## 6. Atlas and Story Use

### Map symbols
- open ring: public gathering site
- three grass strokes: listening field
- shaded crescent: retreat grove
- broken path line: seasonal or movable route
- wind ribbon: weather or migration signal
- empty circle: protected absence or no-entry refuge

### Seasonal events
- **First Wind:** opening of major migration routes
- **Long Table:** inter-region feast with explicit refusal-friendly hospitality
- **Seed Return:** restoration day after heavy use
- **Quiet Horizon:** a low-speech period for grief, regulation, and listening

### Rituals
- **Grass-Listening:** participants hear the land and each other before making a public decision
- **Arrival Without Performance:** newcomers may enter, eat, rest, or observe without introducing themselves
- **The Open Ring:** councils preserve a visible exit and an unoccupied place
- **Name to the Horizon:** grief rite that releases memory without demanding repetition

### Scene engine
**Pressure:** A major arrival feast is underway as a storm front approaches. A quiet visitor refuses the central welcome ritual, while council members argue that public unity requires visible participation.

**Accord gates:**
- **Harmony:** What form of welcome allows belonging to flourish?
- **Midnight:** What vulnerability, fatigue, weather, or history must be honored?
- **Recognition:** Is the visitor rejecting the community, or rejecting a specific demand?

**Repair path:** Move the welcome to a shaded edge, name refusal as valid participation, let the visitor choose witness or privacy, and revise the feast protocol before the next arrival.

### Story anchors
- an arrival feast testing whether refusal can stay welcome
- a grass-listening ritual interrupted by conflicting wind signs
- a migration route closed for restoration during an urgent crossing
- a quiet participant whose objection changes a public council
- a feast whose abundance reveals invisible labor and unequal access

### Connections
- pollinators and seed routes connect the Meadows to Flowering Orchard
- migrating shade and litter exchanges connect it to Nightforest edges
- flood-grass and runoff flow toward Mycelial Marshes
- stories carried from Echoing Caverns return here for public testing
- Beaverkin Way provides conditional passage, repair logistics, and safe-return routes
