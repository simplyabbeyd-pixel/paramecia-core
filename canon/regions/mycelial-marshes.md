# Mycelial Marshes

## 1. Identity
- **Type:** Biome
- **Structural role:** Decomposition, hidden exchange, distributed support, and regenerative processing landscape
- **One-sentence essence:** A wetland of fungal intelligence where decay becomes nourishment, burdens can be redistributed, and no single node is required to carry the whole network.
- **Core question:** How can deep connection nourish everyone without dissolving boundaries or spreading burdens without consent?
- **Canon status:** Regional development complete for Living Atlas Part III; named local details remain revisable.

## 2. Ecological Body

### Terrain and hydrology
The Mycelial Marshes are a shifting wetland of blackwater pools, peat shelves, reed corridors, floating root mats, fungal hummocks, submerged wood, and slow channels that appear and disappear with rainfall. The ground is rarely simply solid or liquid. It is layered, breathing, and responsive to pressure.

Travelers learn to read:
- color changes in water and fungal bloom
- firmness beneath reed beds
- the movement of bubbles and insects
- scent shifts that indicate rot, medicine, contamination, or safe drinking water
- luminous threads that reveal active exchange routes beneath the surface

### Ecological zones
1. **Cleanwater Ribbons:** narrow flowing channels where water remains drinkable and movement is easiest.
2. **Spore Gardens:** raised hummocks cultivated for medicine, food, dyes, fibers, and controlled fungal study.
3. **Rotfields:** active decomposition beds where fallen matter is broken down and returned to the wider ecology.
4. **Memory Pools:** slow, mineral-rich basins where chemical traces, songs, scents, and ritual objects may be preserved.
5. **Dry Islands:** elevated refuges used for rest, quarantine, recovery, and boundary restoration.
6. **Deep Network:** the mostly unseen fungal and root web linking water, soil, plants, structures, and living communities.

### Weather and seasonal change
Fog, warm rain, sudden coldwater surges, summer bloom, drought cracking, and flood pulses continually redraw the marsh. Heavy rain can connect systems that were previously separate. Drought can expose old paths, buried debris, forgotten structures, and contamination once held safely underwater.

### Ecological anchors
- mycelial mats that transfer nutrients without behaving as a single mind
- reeds and sedges that filter water and stabilize channels
- decomposer insects, amphibians, wetland birds, mollusks, and small burrowing kin
- medicinal fungi and dangerous look-alikes requiring trained identification
- peat layers that preserve ecological and cultural traces
- floating gardens grown on woven root rafts
- luminous spore bodies that signal network stress, moisture, or recent disturbance

### Ecological gift
Transformation, nutrient exchange, redundancy, regeneration, distributed memory, and the ability to make new life from what has ended.

### Ecological risk
Contagion, enmeshment, saturation, contamination, boundary loss, untracked burden transfer, and assuming that connection automatically grants access.

## 3. Kin, Culture, and Daily Life

### Kin presence
The Marshes are home to amphibious kin, insect kin, fungi-associated folk, Rootkin, Ratfolk specialists, water-guides, healers, compost stewards, memory tenders, and visitors seeking repair after loss or collapse. No species, guild, or network speaks for the whole wetland.

### Housing
Homes are built on stilts, rafts, dry islands, living root platforms, or movable reed foundations. Structures are designed to detach, drain, or migrate when the marsh shifts. Every dwelling has a clearly marked clean zone, wet zone, and no-transfer boundary.

### Work and craft
- water testing and filtration
- medicine cultivation and fungal identification
- composting, rot stewardship, and safe decomposition
- raft building and root-platform repair
- quarantine support and clean-route maintenance
- burden mapping and network-load assessment
- memory preservation using scent, mineral, fiber, and song
- dye making, fiber weaving, resin work, and fermentation
- tending dry refuges for those who need separation from the network

### Food culture
Meals include fungi, tubers, greens, seeds, fermented foods, marsh grains, aquatic plants, and carefully gathered wetland proteins. Shared dishes are labeled by origin, effect, allergen, preparation method, and whether they carry medicinal or ritual significance.

Receiving food never creates social debt. Refusal is ordinary. Medicine and nourishment are not disguised as one another.

### Art and communication
The Marshes favor woven maps, ripple percussion, reed flutes, scent archives, spore-light patterns, knot records, floating lantern lines, fermentation songs, and temporary sculptures designed to decay back into the wetland.

Communication may move through spoken language, scent, vibration, light, water pattern, touch, written tags, or sealed message vessels. No channel is assumed accessible to everyone.

## 4. Relational Function

The Marshes reveal connection below the visible surface. They teach that support can be distributed without erasing the supported person, and that transformation is not the same as forced assimilation.

### Three Accord reading
- **Harmony — What wants to flourish?** Regeneration, distributed care, reciprocal nourishment, and life emerging from decay.
- **Midnight — What must be honored?** Rot, contamination, grief, illness, saturation, and the costs carried by hidden networks.
- **Recognition — What is actually here?** A living exchange system composed of distinct participants, not inert mud, waste, or one collective organism.

### Regional relational law
- Connection is not permission.
- Contact does not equal transfer consent.
- Shared history does not create permanent access.
- A healer may identify a risk without claiming ownership of another body.
- A network may offer support without requiring disclosure.
- Burdens must be named before they are redistributed.
- Isolation may be care rather than punishment.
- Contamination protocols must not become moral stigma.

### Knowledge layers
- **Public:** cleanwater routes, common crossings, weather conditions, basic hazard markers, open markets.
- **Threshold:** guided medicine gardens, working compost beds, teaching laboratories, shared ritual pools.
- **Witnessed:** grief processing, burden-transfer ceremonies, medical treatment, contamination review, memory retrieval.
- **Protected:** personal health records, private memory stores, quarantine identities, sealed samples, and no-transfer dwellings.

## 5. Consent, Stress, Repair, and Care

### Transfer consent
Any transfer involving medicine, spores, bodily material, memory objects, emotional burden, ritual obligation, or network access requires:
1. a named source
2. a named recipient
3. the thing being transferred
4. the expected effect
5. the duration or endpoint
6. a withdrawal path
7. a containment plan if the transfer goes wrong

Silence, familiarity, urgency, and prior consent are not substitutes for present consent.

### Consent colors in the Marshes
- **Green:** the named exchange may proceed within the stated boundary.
- **Yellow:** slow, reduce contact, clarify effects, or move to a cleaner route.
- **Purple:** curiosity about the exchange, not agreement to receive it.
- **Red:** stop transfer, separate channels, begin containment.
- **White:** rest and no processing requests.
- **Amber:** overload, illness, grief saturation, or aftercare; shift from exchange to stabilization.

Colors may appear on vessel seals, wrist threads, bridge markers, sample tags, lanterns, clothing, spoken language, or tactile tokens.

### Primary stress patterns
- network overload
- absorbing another’s distress without agreement
- assuming communal care requires total openness
- contamination panic becoming social exclusion
- one caretaker becoming the default carrier of every burden
- medicine being offered with hidden expectations
- grief processing becoming compulsory
- confusing empathy with accurate knowledge
- using network data to override personal testimony

### Repair infrastructure

#### Dry refuges
Raised, low-contact spaces provide clean water, warmth, food, rest, sensory quiet, and separation from active fungal or social networks. Entry does not imply illness or wrongdoing.

#### Cleanwater routes
Maintained channels connect clinics, homes, markets, and exits without requiring passage through active rotfields or high-spore zones.

#### Load ledgers
Communities track care burdens by function and duration, not by moral worth. Ledgers reveal when one person, household, guild, or species is carrying disproportionate work.

#### Transfer locks
Physical and symbolic locks interrupt unwanted exchange. They may isolate water flow, close a message channel, seal a memory vessel, suspend ritual access, or halt network participation.

#### Containment circles
Containment is practical, time-limited, and reviewed. It must include care, information, and a route back into ordinary community life.

#### The Untaken Burden
A formal practice allows a person to decline a burden publicly without being framed as selfish, disloyal, or uncaring. The community then redistributes the need or changes the task.

### Conflict and repair practices
- identify what moved, between whom, and under whose authority
- separate harm from contamination stigma
- restore boundaries before demanding reconciliation
- distinguish voluntary support from coerced caretaking
- document emergency transfers and review them afterward
- return memories, samples, tools, and obligations to their rightful holders
- allow clean separation when a relationship or network connection must end
- never require illness, grief, or trauma disclosure as the price of support

## 6. Governance and Stewardship

### Marsh councils
Councils use small circles connected by delegates rather than one enormous assembly. Participants may contribute in person, by sealed note, through a witness, or after a delay. Health and contamination information is shared only at the level necessary for collective safety.

### Stewards
- **Water stewards:** test and protect cleanwater routes.
- **Rot tenders:** manage decomposition beds and nutrient return.
- **Transfer witnesses:** document complex exchanges and confirm consent boundaries.
- **Medicine keepers:** cultivate, identify, prepare, and label medicinal organisms.
- **Dry-island hosts:** maintain low-contact refuge and re-entry support.
- **Load readers:** track hidden labor and recommend redistribution.
- **Memory tenders:** preserve records without claiming authority over their meaning.

Authority remains narrow, reviewable, and revocable. Medical knowledge, network sensitivity, or ecological expertise does not grant general authority over another person.

## 7. Symbols, Rituals, and Seasonal Life

### Map language
- white ripple: cleanwater route
- green-black braid: active mycelial exchange zone
- open circle on a raised line: dry refuge
- sealed vessel: protected memory or sample store
- split reed: no-transfer boundary
- three linked dots with one gap: distributed support with preserved autonomy
- amber lantern: saturation or care station
- red crosshatch: contamination boundary under review
- spiral ending in a seed: decomposition returning to growth

### Seasonal events
- **First Warm Bloom:** inspection of medicines, spore gardens, and flood channels.
- **Flood Joining:** temporary connections are mapped and consent rules renewed as waters merge.
- **Dry-Island Month:** shelters are repaired and communities practice low-contact support.
- **Rot Turning:** compost beds are opened, old materials reviewed, and nutrients returned.
- **Fog Ledger Night:** hidden labor and unseen burdens are named before the next cycle.

### Core rituals
- **Naming the Transfer:** all parts of an exchange are spoken or marked before it begins.
- **Returning the Vessel:** borrowed memory, care tools, ritual objects, or responsibilities are formally returned.
- **The Clean Step:** a threshold washing that marks movement out of overload without shame.
- **Unthreading:** a network connection is carefully ended while preserving what each participant owns.
- **Witnessing the Rot:** grief and endings are acknowledged without demanding immediate meaning or rebirth.

## 8. Routes and Regional Relations

### Great Meadows Grassland
The Marshes receive plant matter, grief processions, healers, and travelers needing quiet recovery after public life. Meadows shelters provide open-air decompression for those leaving saturated wetland systems.

### Flowering Orchard
Compost, pollinator habitat, medicines, fermentation cultures, and water move between the regions. Orchard abundance must not become nutrient extraction from the Marshes.

### Nightforest Canopy
The Rootdark and marsh edge share fungi, nocturnal pollinators, medicine gardens, and hidden water routes. Strict boundaries prevent distributed signaling from entering sanctuary dwellings without consent.

### Echoing Caverns
Mineral water, preserved traces, fungal growth, and subterranean memory practices connect the regions. Echo records and biological memory must remain distinguishable.

### Beaverkin Way
The Way maintains bridges, culverts, locks, ferries, and emergency dry routes. Corridor maintenance does not grant access to protected samples, clinics, memory pools, or private network paths.

## 9. Atlas and Story Use

### Scene engine
A Mycelial Marshes scene should contain:
1. something moving beneath the visible surface
2. an offer of connection, healing, memory, or burden-sharing
3. a boundary that must be named
4. an ecological condition that complicates consent
5. a clean route, dry refuge, or containment option
6. a repair that redistributes rather than merely relocates harm

### Story pressures
- a network begins carrying distress no one knowingly agreed to transmit
- a healer detects contamination but lacks authority to compel treatment
- a dry refuge is accused of encouraging withdrawal from communal life
- one care guild has quietly absorbed too much labor
- a memory pool contains traces from someone who withdrew consent
- floodwater links two previously separate communities
- a medicine works, but carries ritual or ecological costs not initially disclosed
- an apparently isolated resident is actually protecting the wider network from overload

### Regional truth
The Marshes do not teach that everyone is one. They teach that distinct lives can nourish one another without becoming indistinguishable.
