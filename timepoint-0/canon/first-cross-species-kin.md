# Timepoint 0 — First Cross-Species Kin

> Before the paths widen, before the world stirs, they are bound here.

## Canon status

**CANON LOCK — Foundation canon.**

The earliest crosskin did not emerge through migration, diplomacy, bloodline, or formal alliance. They formed where neighboring biomes touched and different peoples grew within the same weather, dangers, foodways, waterways, and seasonal rhythms.

They became kin before anyone had invented a category for it.

Crosskinship begins as a relational, ecological, and cultural category. It is not automatically biological hybridization, magical fusion, ownership, assimilation, or proof that two peoples are essentially the same.

> Not born of blood.  
> Not made by magic.  
> Made by proximity, choice, memory, and deep becoming.

## What makes a crosskin?

A crosskin bond forms when two peoples:

- grow within the same currents of time and place
- share danger, hunger, shelter, work, or wonder
- learn one another’s ways without erasing their own
- create practices neither people possessed alone
- repeatedly choose each other as kin

Crosskinship is a living relation built through proximity, memory, mutual adaptation, and shared becoming.

## The First Crosskin

### 1. Cragsong Crosskin

**Peoples:** Ravenkin + Wolkin  
**Home:** High Crags and Wyvern Peaks  
**Early name:** Stormwhisper

Ravenkin and Wolkin grew beneath the same violent skies. They learned the mountain’s warnings together, sharing lookout ledges, storm shelters, migration signs, and the difficult silence required at great elevation.

Ravenkin taught Wolkin to read distant motion and hidden consequence. Wolkin taught Ravenkin how wind carries intention before sound.

Together they developed the first shared storm-language, using feather angle, ear position, pressure shifts, wingbeat, breath, and stone vibration.

**First bond:** The First Watch. A Wolkin walked beside a Ravenkin into open sky when neither could safely cross alone.  
**Shared gift:** Long-range awareness  
**Shared tension:** Vigilance can harden into suspicion  
**Crosskin principle:** *Watch together, but do not mistake watching for ownership.*

### 2. Gloamvale Crosskin

**Peoples:** Mothkin + Túlkin  
**Home:** Night Forest Canopy  
**Early name:** Luminmoth

Mothkin lived among shadowed flowers, moonlit spores, and the soft chemical language of nocturnal growth. Túlkin moved through high branches, carrying songs, seeds, and observations between distant canopies.

They first bonded by mapping the night winds between leaves. Mothkin perceived light gradients invisible to others. Túlkin heard changes in air and branch-tension before storms arrived.

Together they created suspended lantern-gardens, nocturnal navigation songs, and living canopy paths visible only under moonlight.

**First bond:** The First Shared Song. A Túlkin taught a Mothkin the wind-thread between two trees; the Mothkin revealed the light-path hidden inside it.  
**Shared gift:** Navigation through darkness  
**Shared tension:** One trusts illumination, the other trusts movement  
**Crosskin principle:** *Not all paths must remain visible to remain true.*

### 3. Root-Heart Crosskin

**Peoples:** Dervkin + Bearkin  
**Home:** Heartwood Forest  
**Early name:** Brambleantler

Dervkin and Bearkin were raised around the same root systems, berry grounds, sleeping hollows, and ancient migration corridors. Their friendship grew from protecting the vulnerable edge of the forest together.

Dervkin carried long memory, route knowledge, and sensitivity to disturbance. Bearkin carried physical steadiness, sheltercraft, and a fierce understanding of when intervention was necessary.

They became the earliest guardians of shared forest thresholds, refusing to divide the woods into “theirs” and “ours.”

**First bond:** The First Lesson. A Bearkin taught a young Dervkin the safe-root path between storms. Years later, the Dervkin guided the Bearkin home through a forest altered by flood.  
**Shared gift:** Protection rooted in memory  
**Shared tension:** Protection may become control  
**Crosskin principle:** *Guard what cannot be owned.*

### 4. Wildfur Crosskin

**Peoples:** Foxkin + Catkin  
**Home:** Temperate Woodlands  
**Early name:** Brushstepper

Foxkin and Catkin grew near the same woodland margins, orchard paths, hidden dens, and sun-warmed stone. Their friendship began through curiosity, competition, and repeated encounters at the edge of one another’s territory.

Foxkin brought improvisation, route-making, and questions. Catkin brought precision, patience, and an instinct for the exact moment to act.

Together they developed playful tracking traditions, silent communication games, shared ambush-rescue drills, and a form of woodland diplomacy based on leaving signs without demanding response.

**First bond:** The First Trust. A Catkin slept while a Foxkin kept watch, though neither had yet named the other friend.  
**Shared gift:** Adaptive strategy  
**Shared tension:** Curiosity can trespass; caution can withhold  
**Crosskin principle:** *Learn the boundary well enough to meet at it.*

### 5. Tidebond Crosskin

**Peoples:** Turtlefolk + Otterfolk  
**Home:** Bog-Delta and Coastal Wetlands  
**Early name:** Shellsplash

Turtlefolk and Otterfolk inhabited the same deltas but experienced time differently. Turtlefolk understood endurance, tidal cycles, sediment, and long return. Otterfolk understood play, swift current, improvisation, and openings that lasted only moments.

They became kin while guiding one another through seasonal floods. Their earliest shared settlements combined floating play-platforms, anchored rest-islands, nursery pools, and flood shelters.

**First bond:** The First Play. A Turtlefolk and an Otterfolk chased reflected light through the delta until play revealed a safe current through dangerous water.  
**Shared gift:** Flow held by stability  
**Shared tension:** Patience may feel like refusal; speed may feel careless  
**Crosskin principle:** *Hold the current without stopping it.*

### 6. Sunscale Crosskin

**Peoples:** Snakekin + Lizardkin  
**Home:** Scorched Scrub and Desert-Dry Scrub  
**Early name:** Dunehed *(working spelling pending explicit canon revision)*

Snakekin and Lizardkin shared heat, scarce water, exposed terrain, and the need to read subtle changes in sand, stone, and sun. Their earliest friendship formed around communal shade and the exchange of route-memory.

Snakekin understood vibration, hidden passage, and thermal gradients. Lizardkin understood surface weather, basking cycles, defensive terrain, and the social geography of limited shelter.

Together they developed heat-safe travel formations, buried water markers, sun-clock paths, and agreements regulating access to shade.

**First bond:** The First Offered Food. A young Snakekin and Lizardkin divided the only edible root found during drought.  
**Shared gift:** Survival through exact environmental reading  
**Shared tension:** Secrecy can become exclusion  
**Crosskin principle:** *Scarcity does not excuse abandonment.*

### 7. Webdusk Crosskin

**Peoples:** Batkin + Spiderkin  
**Home:** Echoing Caverns and Cave-Underroot  
**Early name:** Swarmfolk

Batkin and Spiderkin lived within the same dark chambers but mapped them through different senses. Batkin read echo, air current, and moving life. Spiderkin read vibration, tension, contact, and the memory stored in woven structure.

They first became kin by combining echolocation with thread mapping, creating navigational webs that translated air movement into touch.

Their shared chambers became some of the earliest nonvisual archives in the world.

**First bond:** The First Shared Shelter. A Batkin colony sheltered a lost young Spiderkin during a cave collapse. The Spiderkin later wove the supports that kept their roost from falling.  
**Shared gift:** Mapping what cannot be seen  
**Shared tension:** Networks may protect or entrap  
**Crosskin principle:** *A path must remain a path, not become a cage.*

### 8. Mudsong Crosskin

**Peoples:** Swampfolk + Frogfolk  
**Home:** Mycelial Marshes and Swamp-Bog  
**Early name:** Sporebloom

Swampfolk and Frogfolk grew among fungal networks, seasonal pools, reedbeds, decomposition fields, and shifting islands. Neither treated the marsh as inert terrain. They understood it as a layered conversation among water, rot, root, spore, insect, and song.

Frogfolk tracked water quality and weather through voice and skin. Swampfolk understood decomposition, nutrient movement, and the slow intelligence of fungal exchange.

Together they built the first water-filtering gardens and seasonal chorus maps.

**First bond:** The First Waterwork. They built a water-slowing structure that filtered floodwater without severing the marsh’s living routes.  
**Shared gift:** Regeneration through decomposition and song  
**Shared tension:** Purification can become sterilization  
**Crosskin principle:** *Clean water must still remain alive.*

### 9. Wetlands-Edge Crosskin

**Peoples:** Beaverkin + Ratfolk  
**Home:** Riverway and Wetlands Edge

Beaverkin and Ratfolk met where engineered waterways touched dense settlement routes. Beaverkin shaped current, shelter, and long-term hydrology. Ratfolk understood repair, reuse, compact passage, and how to make overlooked materials useful.

Their shared work produced adaptable floodgates, modular bridges, emergency tunnels, and settlements designed to be repaired rather than discarded.

**First bond:** The First Rescue. After a Ratfolk was pulled from a dangerous drain, Beaverkin and Ratfolk redesigned the structure so no one would need rescuing twice.  
**Shared gift:** Practical infrastructure  
**Shared tension:** Efficiency can overlook habitat  
**Crosskin principle:** *Repair the cause, not only the damage.*

### 10. Thornbloom Crosskin

**Peoples:** Hedgekin + Groundhogkin  
**Home:** Hedgerow and Garden Margin  
**Early name:** Briarwhorl

Hedgekin and Groundhogkin grew where cultivated spaces met burrows, root systems, and wild margins. Hedgekin guarded delicate boundaries and understood defensive growth. Groundhogkin understood soil layers, seasonal shelter, excavation, and underground circulation.

Together they created the first inhabited hedgerows, combining aboveground protection with belowground refuge.

**First bond:** The First Orchard. They rooted and burrowed the first shared food margin, protecting blossoms above and water channels below.  
**Shared gift:** Defensive habitat  
**Shared tension:** Protection may harden into isolation  
**Crosskin principle:** *A boundary should shelter life, not imprison it.*

### 11. Nectarbind Crosskin

**Peoples:** Pollinatorkin + Túlkin  
**Home:** Flowering Orchard and Pollinator Guild Zone  
**Early name:** Honeywing

Pollinatorkin and Túlkin became kin through the exchange of pollen, seed, route, song, and seasonal information. Pollinatorkin knew the intimate conditions required for flowering. Túlkin connected distant groves through movement and memory.

Their friendship created the first seasonal bloom routes and shared sky-gardens.

**First bond:** The First Gift. A Pollinatorkin carried sweetness into the cave-underroot; a Túlkin carried the story of it across the canopy.  
**Shared gift:** Distributed abundance  
**Shared tension:** Service may become expectation  
**Crosskin principle:** *A gift is not a debt.*

### 12. Watchkin Crosskin

**Peoples:** Ravenkin + Witness-Path Kin  
**Home:** Silent Threshold  
**Early name:** Gravewitness

At the Silent Threshold, Ravenkin lived beside beings and places shaped by memory, burial, departure, and the keeping of unfinished truths. Their kinship developed through shared vigils rather than shared speech.

They became guardians of passages where presence mattered more than intervention.

**First bond:** The First Promise. Across species and across places, one being said, “I will remember you.” The promise was kept after voice, body, and name had changed.  
**Shared gift:** Continuity through witness  
**Shared tension:** Memory can preserve pain past its season  
**Crosskin principle:** *Remembering must not become possession.*

## First Cross-Species Moments

These events became foundational stories, though later cultures told them differently:

1. **The First Shared Shelter:** One people protected another before treaties or names existed.
2. **The First Offered Food:** Scarcity was answered through division rather than hoarding.
3. **The First Shared Song:** Two different sensory languages found a common pattern.
4. **The First Rescue:** A life was pulled from danger, then the dangerous system itself was redesigned.
5. **The First Lesson:** Knowledge crossed species without demanding sameness.
6. **The First Play:** Joy revealed environmental knowledge that duty had missed.
7. **The First Watch:** Two beings shared vigilance until trust became possible.
8. **The First Gift:** Something valuable crossed a boundary without becoming obligation.
9. **The First Trust:** Vulnerability was offered and not exploited.
10. **The First Promise:** Memory became a chosen responsibility.

## Crosskin Law

Crosskin are not hybrids by default.

They are not a biological merger, a magical fusion, or a replacement species. Crosskin begins as a relational category: people shaped so deeply by neighboring species that their identity, customs, architecture, language, and responsibilities can no longer be explained through one lineage alone.

Some later crosskin may develop shared physical adaptations through long evolutionary continuity. At Timepoint 0, crosskinship is primarily cultural, ecological, and relational.

## Timepoint 0 Design Constraints

Before migration begins:

- crosskin bonds arise only between neighboring or overlapping biomes
- no origin bond depends on distant trade routes
- every relationship emerges from repeated local contact
- each bond produces at least one shared practice
- neither people is absorbed into the other
- crosskinship changes the place as well as the people
- later movement may spread these practices, but it does not create their origin

## Closing Principle

> We are not the same.  
> We are not the one.  
> We are the many, together.

**Many roots. Many paths. Many kin. One worlding.**
